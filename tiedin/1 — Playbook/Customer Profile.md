# Customer Profile

## SECTION 1 — THE CUSTOMER

**1. What kind of founders are these exactly?**

- A guy who bootstrapped a SaaS to $2M ARR, sold it, and now writes about the exact decisions that made it work
- A former operator who ran a 50-person company, burned out, went solo, and now teaches founders how to build leaner
- Someone who spent 15 years in a specific industry, went independent, and now deconstructs how that industry actually works from the inside

**2. What do they write about on Substack?**
Frameworks. Real numbers. Decisions they made and why. The mechanics behind outcomes — hiring, pricing, quitting, scaling, failing. Not theory. Not inspiration. The actual blueprint.

**3. Why do people currently pay them?**
Because the writing saves them time and mistakes. The reader feels like they're getting a senior advisor's thinking for $10/month. It's the cheapest mentorship available.

**4. What's the tone of their writing?**
Analytical with conviction. They don't hedge. They state what they believe, back it with experience, and move on. Think: tactical, direct, occasionally contrarian. Zero performative emotion.

**5. What do they secretly believe about social media?**
That it's a game for people with nothing real to say. That going viral means dumbing things down. That their audience is too smart for TikTok. They're wrong — but that's what they believe. The real belief underneath: "I don't want to embarrass myself on camera."

**6. What do they hate about content creation?**
Performance. The idea that they have to entertain instead of inform. They hate the format pressure — dancing, trending sounds, pointing at text. It feels beneath them. They built real things. They shouldn't have to perform to be taken seriously.

**7. What have they tried before (if anything)?**
Most: nothing. A few have tried hiring a VA to repurpose content — got generic garbage back that didn't sound like them. Some have experimented with LinkedIn posts. Almost none have tried video.

**8. Why didn't it work?**
The VA didn't understand the ideas. The output was surface-level. It lost the voice — the precision, the specificity — that made the writing valuable in the first place. So they stopped.

**9. What's their biggest fear with video content?**
Looking like every other "business guru" on TikTok. They've built authority through depth. Video feels like it demands they trade depth for reach. They're afraid of becoming cringe.

**10. What would make them instantly say "this isn't for me"?**
Seeing a generic template-looking video. Trending audio. Motivational tone. Anything that doesn't sound exactly like them. The moment it feels like a content mill, they're out.

## SECTION 2 — THE PAIN

**1. When do they feel the frustration most?**
Right after hitting publish on a post they're genuinely proud of. They refresh the stats. 200 opens. 40 clicks. And they know — somewhere — that post deserved 10,000 people to read it. That moment is the pain.

**2. What thought runs through their head when they hit publish?**
"This is good. Nobody is going to see it."

**3. What's the real emotional pain?**
Invisibility. They've done something real. They have the scars and the frameworks to prove it. And they're writing into a void. The subscriber count feels like a verdict on their relevance — and it's wrong.

**4. What are they losing every month because of this?**
Compounding subscribers they'll never get back. Revenue that could have been recurring. Readers who would have paid — who are currently following some louder, less qualified person on Instagram instead.

**5. Who are they comparing themselves to?**
The people with 50K subscribers who write worse than them. The loud ones. The ones who package mediocre thinking in slick formats and win algorithmically. It drives them insane.

**6. What pisses them off about those people?**
They're winning on style over substance. The wrong people are getting the audience. And our writer knows it — but doesn't know how to fix it without becoming them.

**7. What's the opportunity they KNOW they're missing?**
Video. They know it. They've watched the numbers. They just haven't found a way to do it that doesn't require them to compromise the work or spend 10 hours a week they don't have.

**8. What's the cost of doing nothing for 6 months?**
The same 2,000 subscribers. The same revenue plateau. Six months of writing great posts that compound for nobody. While someone else with half the insight builds an audience three times the size.

## SECTION 3 — THE DESIRE

**1. What does "success" look like for them in 90 days?**
A visible, measurable jump in paid subscribers — something they can point to and say the writing is finally reaching the people it deserves to reach. +30 to +100 new paid subs from a channel they didn't operate themselves.

**2. Do they care more about money, audience, or authority?**
Authority first. Audience second. Money follows. They already have some revenue. What they want is the reach that validates the work.

**3. What would make them feel like "this is working"?**
New subscribers showing up in their inbox citing a video they saw on TikTok or Instagram. Proof that the distribution gap is closing.

**4. What's the dream scenario (be specific)?**
Their best post from two years ago — the one with the real framework, the one they worked on for a week — gets turned into a video that quietly accumulates 200K views over six months and adds 400 subscribers. The writing finally has a lifespan longer than the day it was published.

**5. What would they brag about if this worked?**
"I added 200 paid subscribers last quarter without writing a single extra word or touching social media once."

## SECTION 4 — THE SERVICE

**1. Walk me step-by-step: what happens after they submit the quiz?**
They submit name and email. I get notified. I go to their Substack, read their last 3 free posts, find the one with the strongest single idea, write a script from their words, produce a fully branded faceless video, and deliver it to their inbox within 2 days.

**2. How do you actually turn writing into video?**
I take their best idea from a post. Distill it to a hook, a framework, a payoff — 45 to 90 seconds. Write a tight script using their exact language and voice. Source visuals that match their brand style from the quiz. Produce it using generative AI tools I've spent months mastering. Output: a polished faceless video that sounds and looks like an extension of their newsletter, not a different product.

**3. What makes your videos different from generic ones?**
The script comes from their actual ideas — not a generic "5 tips" template. The visual style is determined by their quiz responses — matched specifically to their brand. The voice stays theirs. The specificity stays intact. It's not repurposed content. It's their content in a new format.

**4. What do you do that removes effort from them?**
Everything. They don't write the script. They don't choose the visuals. They don't edit. They don't post. The only thing they do is confirm they like it — one reply to an email.

**5. What do you NOT do?**
Write original content. Manage their social accounts. Run ads. Touch anything outside converting their existing writing into video.

**6. What's the fastest they see something tangible?**
2 days from quiz submission to video in inbox.

**7. What control do they have?**
Full approval before anything goes live. They see the video before it's posted anywhere. One round of feedback included.

## SECTION 5 — PRICING & RISK

**1. What are you planning to charge?**

- The Bet: $0/mo + 40% commission on new paid subscriber revenue
- The Partnership: $200/mo + 20% commission
- The Engine: $1,000/mo flat, no commission

**2. Why that price?**
The $200 flat covers model costs for 10 videos. The commission aligns incentives completely — I only make real money if they grow. The $1,000 tier exists to show what the commission is actually worth in cash terms.

**3. What makes it obviously worth more than that?**
10 videos/month from any other video production service costs $500–$2,000 minimum. These are branded, scripted, idea-driven — not generic. At $200 + commission you're paying $20/video with zero upside risk.

**4. What's the closest alternative they'd compare this to?**
Hiring a freelance video editor or content agency. Those charge $50–$200 per video, upfront, regardless of results. No skin in the game.

**5. Why is your way better?**
I eat the risk. If the videos don't grow their subscribers, the commission is zero. They paid $200 for 10 videos and walked away. No agency offers that.

## SECTION 6 — QUIZ ENTRY PAGE

**1. Why a quiz instead of a normal form?**
A form asks for commitment before proof of quality. The quiz IS the proof — by the time they submit their email, they've already seen the range of what's possible through the style collages. The quiz earns the email instead of demanding it.

**2. What's the promise of the free video?**
A fully produced, branded, idea-driven short-form video made from their own writing — delivered to their inbox in 2 days. No cost. No commitment.

**3. What makes someone actually want to take the quiz?**
The collages in the background communicate quality before a word is read. The premise is irresistible: a free video, already being made, styled to them specifically. Two minutes to see what's possible.

**4. What's the curiosity hook?**
"What would your ideas look like as video?" The quiz answers that question visually before the video is even made.

**5. What would make someone hesitate before starting it?**
Thinking it's a disguised sales funnel. Worrying it'll take longer than promised. Not believing the free video is actually free. The confirmation screen addresses all three directly.
